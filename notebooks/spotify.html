From Valence to Virality: The Science Behind Streaming Hits - A Spotify analysis¶

Introduction to coding and data management part 2 - Alessia Rustignoli¶

Introduction¶

This project explores the dynamics of music streaming by analyzing Spotify data to uncover what makes songs successful in the digital age. By examining audio features, genre trends, artist performance, and listener behavior, we reveal how streaming platforms shape, and are shaped by modern music consumption. The analysis focuses on three key questions: What sonic characteristics dominate streaming? How do genres and artists compete for attention? And how do seasonal and algorithmic factors influence what we listen to?

The data reveals clear patterns: pop and rap lead in streams, driven by high energy, danceability, and short runtimes optimized for playlists. Latin music and R&B emerge as strong seasonal contenders, while rock and EDM underperform despite their cultural presence. At the artist level, figures like Post Malone, Ed Sheeran, and Bad Bunny dominate by blending genre appeal with platform-friendly formulas, yet even their success fluctuates month-to-month, reflecting streaming’s fast-paced cycles.

Beyond raw numbers, this project highlights the tension between artistic expression and algorithmic logic. Songs classified as "happy" or "angry" thrive, while "chill" tracks struggle, not necessarily due to listener preference alone, but because of how platforms prioritize engagement. The findings suggest that streaming doesn’t just reflect taste; it actively shapes it, creating a feedback loop where data-driven trends influence future production.

By connecting audio features, commercial performance, and listener habits, this analysis offers a nuanced look at the forces defining music’s digital era.

About the datasets¶

  1. spotify_songs.csv: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/joebeachcapital/30000-spotify-songs?select=spotify_songs.csv

This dataset contains information on about 30000 Spotify songs, taken directly from Spotify's API. In order to have a better comprehension of the notebook content, it is important to specify what some technical terms refer to:

  • Danceability describes how suitable a track is for dancing based on a combination of musical elements including tempo, rhythm stability, beat strength, and overall regularity. A value of 0.0 is least danceable and 1.0 is most danceable.
  • Energy is a measure from 0.0 to 1.0 and represents a perceptual measure of intensity and activity. Typically, energetic tracks feel fast, loud, and noisy. For example, death metal has high energy, while a Bach prelude scores low on the scale. Perceptual features contributing to this attribute include dynamic range, perceived loudness, timbre, onset rate, and general entropy.
  • Speechiness detects the presence of spoken words in a track. The more exclusively speech-like the recording (e.g. talk show, audio book, poetry), the closer to 1.0 the attribute value. Values above 0.66 describe tracks that are probably made entirely of spoken words. Values between 0.33 and 0.66 describe tracks that may contain both music and speech, either in sections or layered, including such cases as rap music. Values below 0.33 most likely represent music and other non-speech-like tracks.
  • Instrumentalness predicts whether a track contains no vocals. “Ooh” and “aah” sounds are treated as instrumental in this context. Rap or spoken word tracks are clearly “vocal”. The closer the instrumentalness value is to 1.0, the greater likelihood the track contains no vocal content. Values above 0.5 are intended to represent instrumental tracks, but confidence is higher as the value approaches 1.0.
  • Liveness detects the presence of an audience in the recording. Higher liveness values represent an increased probability that the track was performed live. A value above 0.8 provides strong likelihood that the track is live.
  • Acousticness is a confidence measure from 0.0 to 1.0 of whether the track is acoustic. 1.0 represents high confidence the track is acoustic.
  • Valence is a measure from 0.0 to 1.0 describing the musical positiveness conveyed by a track. Tracks with high valence sound more positive (e.g. happy, cheerful, euphoric), while tracks with low valence sound more negative (e.g. sad, depressed, angry).
  • Tempo is the overall estimated tempo of a track in beats per minute (BPM). In musical terminology, tempo is the speed or pace of a given piece and derives directly from the average beat duration.
  1. spotify_top_songs_audio_features.csv: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/julianoorlandi/spotify-top-songs-and-audio-features

This dataset contains information about all the songs that were in Spotify top 200 charts since 2016. In addition to the number of streams and the number of weeks in the charts, it contains information about the audio features of each track, such as tempo, key and danceability. The source of this technical information is the Spotify web API.

# Define the mood function
def mood(valence, energy):
    if valence > 0.5 and energy > 0.5:
        return "Happy"
    elif valence < 0.5 and energy > 0.5:
        return "Angry"
    elif valence < 0.5 and energy < 0.5:
        return "Sad"
    else:
        return "Chill"

Conclusions¶

This comprehensive analysis of Spotify streaming data reveals a music ecosystem shaped by algorithmic patterns, cultural moments, and listener psychology, where sonic characteristics, artist strategies, and platform mechanics intersect to create today's hit-making formula. The dominance of short, high-energy pop and rap tracks with strong danceability metrics confirms how streaming rewards immediate gratification, with songs like "Shape of You" and "The Box" achieving ubiquity by optimizing for playlist inclusion. Yet beneath this surface-level homogeneity lies fascinating nuance: Latin music's consistent strong performance reflects globalization's impact, while R&B's seasonal resurgence proves emotional resonance still follows calendar cycles, even in our always-on streaming era. The stark genre hierarchies, with pop towering over rock and EDM underperforming relative to its live presence, highlight how streaming platforms have recalibrated genre power structures, privileging certain production styles (tight structures, vocal-forward mixes) over others.

Artist-level data exposes the tension between platform optimization and authentic expression. Post Malone and Ed Sheeran's dominance stems from their ability to bridge algorithmic preferences with mass appeal, while Billie Eilish's success with lower-energy "sad" tracks proves there's space for counter-currents. The seasonal volatility in monthly rankings, where no artist maintains year-round dominance, illustrates streaming's accelerated hype cycles, where attention spans shorten and even megastars must constantly reassert relevance.

Most revealing is the infrastructure beneath these trends: the winner-takes-all distribution among labels mirrors the concentration at artist and track levels, suggesting streaming hasn't truly democratized music so much as reshaped the gatekeepers. That niche subgenres like trap and reggaeton now outperform legacy categories (rock, jazz) speaks to how algorithmic discovery rewards distinctive sonic signatures, but primarily those adaptable to playlist formats. Ultimately, these patterns depict an industry where success requires understanding streaming as both culture and infrastructure: the "happy" uptempo songs dominating moods aren't just what listeners want, but what the platform's reward system incentivizes creators to make.
